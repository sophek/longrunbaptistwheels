EARLY BAPTISTS AGAINST SLAVERY Most people have read of the fiery preaching of Wesley and Finney against slavery. Yet, many are not aware of the extent that BAPTISTS also reproved the sin. In 1652, Rhode Island passed the first law in North America against slavery. BAPTIST preacher, John Clark (who founded the FIRST Baptist church on America soil), received a Royal Charter for Rhode Island in 1663. It has been said that no individual since Roger Williams ("Baptistic" preacher) is better known as a champion of religious liberty than Isaac Backus (1704-1806). For his work and energy, he has also been called the "father of American Baptists." He was the most influential Baptist leader in Colonial America. In 1785, he wrote, "Godliness excludes slavery, in Answer to John Cleaveland." In 1805, a year before his death, he wrote the following words: "The liberty that he [Roger Williams] was for, civil and religious, is now enjoyed in thirteen of the seventeen of the United States of America. No tax for any religious minister is imposed by authority in any of the said 13 states, and their power is weakened in the other 4. SLAVERY is also abolished in the northern states and it is wearing away in the rest..." (Letter to Elder William Richards, April 20th, 1805) Backus preached in the homes of blacks. In 1773, Backus received a letter from a fellow Baptist in condemnation of slavery, which he then read to his church: "I take liberty to offer to your consideration one thing that I verily believe ought to be razed out of the church of Christ: and that is the making slaves of the poor Negroes....Though I know of but few in our [Baptist] churches that have Negro slaves, yet the thing is allowed and not witnessed against as I think it ought to be...That it appears to me high time for the watchmen to sound an alarm against this practice, and for churches to purge themselves from these..." (Ebenzer Smith to Isaac Backus, 1773) The following year, Backus praised the Continental Congress for their concern over slavery. On one occasion Backus wrote: "No man abhors that wicked practice [of slavery] more than I do, and would gladly make use of lawful means towards the abolishing of slavery in all parts of the land. But let us consider where we are, and what we are doing. In the Articles of Confederation no provision was made to hinder the importation of slaves into any of these states; but a door is now open hereafter to do it, and each state is at liberty now to abolish slavery as soon as they please....Slavery grows more odious through the world; and as an honorable gentleman said some days ago, 'Though we cannot say that slavery is struck with an apolexy, yet we may hope it will die with consumption." In 1787, the Warren (Baptist) Association (Rhode Island), after referring to the slave trade as "menstealing," stated: "We therefore earnestly desire all our brethren to guard against giving the least countenance to this Heaven daring wickedness." In 1787, the Kentockian Association (Virginia) stated: "...hereditary slavery was a breach of the divine law." It appointed a committee to form a plan for "gradual emancipation." In 1789, the Philadelphia Baptist Association (urged by the First Baptist Church of Baltimore) praised existing abolition societies, and recommended that its churches form similar societies: "...this Association declares their high approbation of the several societies formed in the United States and Europe, for the gradual abolition of the slavery of the Africans....and do hereby recommend to the churches we represent to form similar societies to become members thereof and exert themselves to obtain this important object." John Leland (1754-1841) was a friend to Thomas Jefferson and James Madison. Against the evils of slavery, he wrote: "The whole scene of slavery is pregnant with enormous evils....why not liberate them at once? Would to Heaven this were done! The sweets of rural and social life will never be well enjoyed, until it is the case." In 1790, he proposed a resolution adopted by the Baptist General Committee of Virginia. It read: "Resolved, That slavery is a violent deprivation of the rights of nature and inconsistent with a republican government, and therefore recommend it to our brethren to use every legal measure to extirpate this horrid evil from the land; and pray Almighty God that our honorable Legislature may have it in their power to proclaim the great Jubilee consistent with the principles of good policy." In 1792 the General Committee passed another, softer resolution declaring that the issue of slavery belonged to the legislative body. John Asplund (1755-1807), an early chronicler of Baptist history, in 1791, wrote against, "keeping our fellow creatures in bondage, who had as good a right as we, both to civil and religious liberty." In 1792, the Shaftesbury Baptist Association (Vermont) stated: "That this association being impressed with a sense of that freedom which every child of Adam is entitled to by nature, and which they cannot be deprived but by hostile usurpation, take this method of manifesting their hearty detestation of the slave trade, and recommend it to our brethren to pray Almighty God, to hasten the auspicious day when the Ethiopian with all the human race shall enjoy the liberty due to every good citizen of the commonwealth, and the name of slave be extirpated from the earth." John Clark (d.1833) was a Baptist preacher who moved to Illinois, where he joined, "The Baptized Church of Christ, Friends of Humanity," and became active in the anti-slavery movement. In 1797, the Dover Baptist Association (Virginia) stated: "We sincerely sympathize, both as Christians and citizens, with these unhappy people [black slaves], and although we think it a delicate matter, we would not wish to be backward in promoting their happiness and liberty, upon cautious ground. We would, therefore, recommend to our Brethren to unite with the Abolition Societies in proposing a petition to the General Assembly for their gradual emancipation upon some rational and benevolent plan." In 1807, the Elkhorn Baptist Association (Kentucky) divided over slavery, and Baptist preachers David Barrow and Carter Tarrant, led in the formation of the "Baptized Licking Locust Association, Friends of Humanity," which was dedicated to preaching against slavery. In 1807, the Miami Baptist Association, in regard to its churches, urged that, "caution be used in admitting among them those who held the sentiment and practice of hereditary slavery." In 1808, the New York Baptist Association stated: "In the opinion of this association the practice of slaveholding ought to be discountenanced as much as possible." In 1815, the Sandy Creek Baptist Association (North Carolina) condemned the buying and selling of slaves for profit. In 1818, the Chowan Baptist Association (North Carolina) condemned the buying and selling of slaves for profit. In 1815, the Cumberland River Baptist Association (Kentucky) stated: "We conceive that all nations, by nature, have a right to equal freedom....We think it would be best to wait for the dispensations of Providence, and pray to God for the happy year of their [the black slaves] deliverance to commence." In 1835, English Baptists James Hoby and Francis Cox sailed to America to admonish American Baptists to abolish slavery. In 1835, the Sandy Creek Association (North Carolina) again stood against the "selling and buying of slaves." By 1835, two-thirds of the delegates to the meeting of the Anti-Slavery Society (in New England) were BAPTIST and Methodist preachers. In the North, opposition to slavery grew increasingly fervent. Northern Baptist fellowships began to forbid its members to have any fellowship with slaveholders. In 1836, the Hancock Baptist Association (Maine) called for immediate emancipation. In 1837, popular Baptist preacher, William Henry Brisbane, left South Carolina due to his stand against slavery. (Meanwhile, Alexander Campbell, the "Church of Christ" preacher, was stayed busy writing tracts and articles in defense of slavery). In 1839, Freewill Baptists stated: "Resolved, That it is with sorrow of heart that we learn that slavery is tolerated, practiced, and defended in certain portions of the Christian church." South Carolina and North Carolina Baptists allowed blacks to become members of their churches, and they tried to greatly improve the condition of slaves. Yet, in the face of fierce opposition, they felt compelled to DEFEND the institution of slavery against the "meddling Northern abolitionists." In 1840, the American Baptist Anti-Slavery Convention met in New York and caused great agitation. By 1845, American Baptists finally split over slavery, and the Southern Baptist Convention was born.
