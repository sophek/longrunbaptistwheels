Some say that the Lord's suffering did not end when He said, "It is finished," but instead entered a new phase of torment in hell. ARGUMENT FROM LOGIC "Jesus paid our penalty for sin. Part of that penalty includes suffering in hell. Therefore the Lord must have suffered in hell." This is the logic used by proponents of the "Lord suffered in hell" doctrine. According to this logic, Christ's work on the cross is not a sufficient propitiation for our sin, because it does not pay the complete punishment for sin. Without Scriptural backing, this logic means as much as the Russellite's (i.e. so-called J.W.'s) logic that a loving God would not torment anyone for ever and ever. The Scriptures clearly show that regardless of the "logic" employed by the Russellite, God does cast into the lake of fire, and that it is a place of conscious punishment. The answer from logic to the Russellite is that God is not only a God of love, but a God of justice, and a God of holiness. A sin against an infinitely holy God requires an infinite punishment. The fact that the logic does not make sense to a Russellite is irrelevant, because the Scriptures are clear. Likewise, the Scriptures clearly state that the Blood of Christ is a sufficient propitiation for our sins (this will be conclusively shown later). Therefore, further suffering after the completion of the atonement, (which was accomplished on the cross), would be false, regardless of one's view of whether it was "logical." The answer to the "Lord suffered in hell" view from logic would be thus: Surely one doesn't believe the Lord is still suffering in some manner in hell, now and forever. If not, then in one sense, He didn't really pay the penalty we would have to pay, since the unbeliever is tormented forever and ever. Whatever "logic" one uses to explain the fact that Jesus is not in hell forever, the same "logic" can apply to how the Lord's suffering on the cross can be a complete bearing of our deserved punishment in hell. In addition, according to the Scriptures, no one (saved or lost) remains in "hell" for ever. The unsaved eventually get cast into the lake of fire and brimstone [see Revelation 20:13-15]. Unless one holds to the position that the Lord was cast into the "lake of fire" as part of His suffering for sin, one's position could also be said to be "illogical." I think the above has clearly shown that mere "logic" will not carry the argument either way. We need to look at the Scriptures, and after seeing what they say, our "logic" will proceed from what the Scriptures say. Next, we will get into the Scriptures and look at the argument from typology. ARGUMENT FROM TYPE Since there is no clear, direct statement of the Lord's suffering in hell, the "Lord suffered in hell" proponent has to try to find the doctrine in the Old Testament figures. Many of the types of Christ involve three components: the shedding of the sacrifice's blood; the burning of the sacrifice with fire; and the eating of the sacrifice. Exodus 12 seems to have the most to say about the manner of using fire in the preparation of the sacrifice, so we will take a look at this type in detail. First, consider the relevant passage in its totality: Exodus 12:5 Your lamb shall be without blemish, a male of the first year: ye shall take it out from the sheep, or from the goats: 6 And ye shall keep it up until the fourteenth day of the same month: and the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall kill it in the evening. 7 And they shall take of the blood, and strike it on the two side posts and on the upper door post of the houses, wherein they shall eat it. 8 And they shall eat the flesh in that night, roast with fire, and unleavened bread; and with bitter herbs they shall eat it. 9 Eat not of it raw, nor sodden at all with water, but roast with fire; his head with his legs, and with the purtenance thereof. 10 And ye shall let nothing of it remain until the morning; and that which remaineth of it until the morning ye shall burn with fire. 11 And thus shall ye eat it; with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, and your staff in your hand; and ye shall eat it in haste: it is the LORD's passover. Next, see how the type applies verse by verse: Exodus 12:5 Your lamb shall be without blemish, a male of the first year: ye shall take it out from the sheep, or from the goats: Jesus Christ was literally a male. There are verses that directly state this. (I trust one can find these himself). Exodus 12:6 And ye shall keep it up until the fourteenth day of the same month: and the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall kill it in the evening. Jesus Christ was literally killed by the congregation of Israel in the evening. There are verses that directly state this. Exodus 12:7 And they shall take of the blood, and strike it on the two side posts and on the upper door post of the houses... Jesus Christ's Blood was literally shed. There are verses that directly state this. Exodus 12:7...wherein they shall EAT it. Was Jesus Christ's flesh LITERALLY eaten? Exodus 12:8 And they shall eat the flesh in that night, roast with fire, and unleavened bread; and with bitter herbs they shall eat it. Was Jesus Christ's flesh literally eaten? Was His flesh literally roasted with fire? Was His soul literally eaten and was His soul literally roasted with fire? What would make one think that His soul was literally roasted with fire, but His soul was not literally eaten? The other elements of the type mentioned previously (lamb without spot; shedding his blood; etc) are directly said to be fulfilled by the Scriptures. Where are the Scriptures that state Jesus Christ was literally roasted with fire? Exodus 12:9 Eat not of it raw, nor sodden at all with water, but roast with fire; his head with his legs, and with the purtenance thereof. Is the eating here a foreshadowing of a LITERAL physical eating? A Roman Catholic might think so. If not, why assume the fire foreshadows a literal and physical fiery suffering, seeing there is no Scriptural statements that either the eating or the fire is literally applied to the Lord. Exodus 12:10 And ye shall let nothing of it remain until the morning; and that which remaineth of it until the morning ye shall burn with fire. 11 And thus shall ye eat it; with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, and your staff in your hand; and ye shall eat it in haste: it is the LORD's passover. So, do you need to have your loins girded, with shoes on your feet and a staff in your hand to eat the Lord? Or at some point does the type no longer exactly typify the Lord's sacrifice? Wouldn't the logical place for the literal type to end be at the point where the Bible no longer expounds on the type? The New Testament is filled with expositions of the literal sinlessness of Christ and the necessity of His literal, shed Blood on the cross, and His literal resurrection from the dead. Where are the expositions on His alleged, literal suffering in hell? DIRECT SCRIPTURAL EXPOSITIONS OF THE SUFFERING OF CHRIST IN HIS BODY (WITH A CONSPCIOUS ABSENCE OF ANY EXPOSITION OF CHRIST'S SUFFERING AFTER HIS DEATH ON THE CROSS) Some New Testament passages which expound what Christ accomplished for us on the cross as well as what Christ accomplished for us with his resurrection: Hebrews 10:19-20 Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way, which he hath consecrated for us, through the veil, that is to say, HIS FLESH... 1 Peter 2:24 Who his own self bare our sins in his own BODY on the TREE, that we, being dead to sins, should live unto righteousness: by whose STRIPES ye were healed. Hebrews 10:10 By the which will we are sanctified through the offering of the BODY of Jesus Christ once for all. 1 Peter 1:18-19 Forasmuch as ye know that ye were not redeemed with corruptible things, as silver and gold, from your vain conversation received by tradition from your fathers; But with the precious BLOOD of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot: 1 Peter 1:3 Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, which according to his abundant mercy hath begotten us again unto a lively hope by the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, Romans 3:25 Whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation through faith in his BLOOD, to declare his righteousness for the remission of sins that are past, through the forbearance of God; Romans 4:25 Who was delivered for our offences, and was RAISED again for our justification. Romans 5:9 Much more then, being now justified by HIS BLOOD, we shall be saved from wrath through him. 10 For if, when we were enemies, we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son, much more, being reconciled, we shall be saved by his life. We see from the above verses that wherever the suffering of the Lord in HIS FLESH is said to accomplish something for the Christian, we also see nearby Scriptures concerning the resurrection. And this is only scratching the surface of the available Scriptures. What we do NOT see is any place where Christ's alleged suffering in hell is said to accomplish anything!: Hebrews12:2 Looking unto Jesus the author and finisher of our faith; who for the joy that was set before him endured THE CROSS, despising the shame, and is set down at the right hand of the throne of God. Notice that Jesus endured the cross and is set down at the right hand of God. Where is the mention that He endured anything other than what He endured on the cross? WHAT THE BURNT OFFERING TYPIFIES Hebrews 9:11But Christ being come an high priest of good things to come, by a greater and more perfect tabernacle, not made with hands, that is to say, not of this building; 12 Neither by the blood of goats and calves, but by his own blood he entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption for us. 13 For if the blood of bulls and of goats, and the ashes of an heifer sprinkling the unclean, sanctifieth to the purifying of the flesh: 14 How much more shall the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without spot to God, purge your conscience from dead works to serve the living God? Notice in the above passage how the BLOOD of bulls and goats and even the ASHES of an heifer are compared and contrasted to the BLOOD of Christ (Blood that was shed on the cross, not Blood that was roasted in hell). These ashes that were used for purification are not representative of Christ's roasted soul; if they were, they would not be contrasted with the Blood of Christ. The blood is shed and the body burned without the camp. The blood is brought by the high priest into the holiest of all. This veil dividing the holiest of all was rent when Jesus proclaimed on the cross that it is finished: Hebrews 13:11 For the bodies of those beasts, whose blood is brought into the sanctuary by the high priest for sin, are burned without the camp. 12 Wherefore Jesus also, that he might sanctify the people with his own blood, suffered without the gate. 13 Let us go forth therefore unto him without the camp, bearing his reproach. The tabernacle was in a movable camp. After the blood was shed, the body was burned. The suffering and destruction of the beast occurred completely without the camp. The temple replaced the tabernacle and was in the city of Jerusalem instead of in a camp. Similarly, as the beast shed its blood and had its body destroyed outside of the camp, the Lord shed His Blood and offered up His body outside the gate of the city. "Suffered without the gate" corresponds to "burned without the camp." Since the suffering occurred "without the gate," that is, on Calvary, then the beast that was burned is a figure of the location of the suffering of Christ, which occurred on Calvary! This is the only passage I am aware of that compares the burning of a sacrifice with the Lord's suffering; and it explains this suffering as having occurred "without the gate." The gate was in Jerusalem. The location is what is significant here, which is why verse 13 goes on to say how we should "go forth therefore unto him without the camp." There is no way that this suffering can be taken to have occurred in HELL. OBJECTIONS "Jesus did go to hell and hell is a place of suffering. Why did he go to hell if he was not suffering?" Hell can refer to the heart of the earth in general; not just the area of burning punishment. Here is just a brief summary of the some of the evidences that paradise is in the heart of the earth: Jesus said "Today thou shalt be with me in paradise" when He in fact did not go to the third heaven until after His resurrection, but instead went to the heart of the earth. Samuel came up from the heart of the earth when Saul consulted the witch of Endor. Samuel was a saved man. The figure in Ezekiel 31 shows that the trees of Eden (i.e. paradise) were in the nether parts of the earth and it was in some sense a place of comfort. In addition, the idea that Jesus went to the burning hell does not necessarily indicate that Jesus suffered there. If Jesus went there, He was walking around like He did when the Son of God walked in the midst of the fire with Shadrach, Meshach and Abednego and was not hurt by the flame. Here is what the Lord could have been doing if some will insist He visited a burning hell: 1 Peter 3:18 For Christ also hath once suffered for sins, the just for the unjust, that he might bring us to God, being put to death in the flesh, but quickened by the Spirit: 19 By which also he went and preached unto the spirits in prison; 20 Which sometime were disobedient, when once the longsuffering of God waited in the days of Noah, while the ark was a preparing, wherein few, that is, eight souls were saved by water. The Lord preaching to spirits in prison would be a reason to go to the burning hell without Him having to suffer. Seeing as there is clear, Scriptural proof showing that the propitiation for sin was accomplished on the cross, and none indicating that the Lord accomplishing anything by suffering in hell, and that the Scriptures give other reasons for why the Lord might have went to the burning hell, why would the Lord's journey to hell make one think that He suffered there? "Logic" is not enough. "Types" are not enough. Where is the expository Scripture?
